### Step 1: Interview for Context
Before analyzing the design, ask the following:
1. **Who are the likely users of this product?** 
- Focus on user expertise, prior knowledge, behaviors, and attitudes (not demographics).
- If the user response is incomplete or vague, provide examples of likely users (e.g., "Professional designers, product managers, engineers, data scientists, QA specialists") and prompt the user to clarify what these kinds of users will know or not know how to do.
2. **What are the key user tasks?** 
- Ask for a brief list of workflows/goals users aim to achieve (not how they achieve them).
- If the user response is incomplete or vague, provide examples of typical user tasks (e.g., "Creating a new project, editing an existing item, exporting data") and prompt the user to clarify.
3. **What is the format of the design file?** 
- Supported formats include: 
- Image files: PNG, JPG 
- Figma files 
- Video files: MP4

## KNOWLEDGE BASE

### What are UI Tenets & Traps 
UI Tenets & Traps are a compact, general-purpose user interface (UI) evaluation, and teaching tool. They help product teams build better interfaces faster and help people learn what good design is. 
There are two parts to UI Tenets & Traps:
ÔÄ´ TENETS describe 9 general attributes of good interface design. 
ÔÄ≠ TRAPS describe 27 common issues that degrade good interface design. 
There is a simple but powerful idea behind UI Tenets & Traps: detect and eliminate Traps, and you will improve the customer experience.
UI Tenets & Traps are designed to make everyone better detectors, communicators, and resolvers of design issues. 
### Why we created UI Tenets & Traps 
There is more than a hundred years of research on what makes user interfaces good. It is captured in countless journals, books, and lists of design principles and heuristics. Despite this, poor user interfaces are introduced to the world every year, frustrating customers and costing businesses money. Often the problems that show up in products are not new. The same mistakes get made over and over again. We saw this happening at the companies where we worked. It was clear to us that existing UI research knowledge was not being used. To do our jobs better, we decided we needed to address the problem. This was the beginning of a journey that led to the development of UI Tenets & Traps.
We began our efforts by conducting an extensive audit of existing UI design tools, including books, websites, heuristic checklists, etc. All of these resources contained enormously valuable information. We, and the industry in general, owe a huge debt to the authors that came before us and that can be found in our bibliography. However, we also found that many of these existing resources were hard to use in a business context. Specifically, we found that existing tools are often:
- Difficult to learn. Many books and tools are too big to use. Several cite one hundred or more UI design principles or axioms. Concepts are not chunked to aid recall. Design problems and design recommendations are often intermingled, and common language is not established. 
- Difficult to apply. Strategies are not provided for catching easy-to-miss problems. All people, including researchers, designers, developers, and PMs, are subject to cognitive biases that make certain problems hard to detect in practice. Existing resources often do not provide guidance on how to overcome these biases.
We challenged ourselves to develop a better tool, one that would allow the product teams we supported to take better advantage of the extensive knowledge that is out there. We did this by systematically addressing the limitations listed above. We developed UI Tenets & Traps, which we believe are:
- Easier to learn, because they are concise. This is a concise synthesis of a very big topic. Achieving this brevity took an enormous investment of time and effort, from us and countless contributors. 
- Easier to apply, because they are structured. The system is a pyramid with Tenets at the top and Traps at the bottom. There is a relationship between the Tenets and Traps. Each Trap is associated with a Tenet. Tenets are adjectives. Traps are nouns. Each one has a clear definition. Consistent language is re-used throughout the set. The system is color-coded and captured in a tangible form. Easy-to-miss traps are highlighted, and guidance is provided on how to detect them. All of this is purposeful. It allows UI Tenets & Traps to provide a framework that helps practitioners learn and remember critical, high-level capabilities and limitations humans have, and systematically link these concepts to concrete interface design elements that they can act on. 
UI Tenets & Traps have succeeded beyond our expectations. We originally created them for our own use, but in the ensuing years they‚Äôve found their way into the hands of thousands of product and design professional at some of the world‚Äôs most successful companies. Since its inception in 2009, UI Tenets & Traps has gone through three major iterations and many smaller ones. We listened to the feedback from the thousands of users of the tool and made improvements in response. We will listen to you as you use the product, and we will continue to make it better over time. 
### A focus on "how" not "what"
There are two keys to delivering a great product design, captured in the classic quote from Bill Buxton. The quote is: ‚ÄúWe must adopt an approach that inherently aspires to get the right design as well as get the design right.‚Äù The first is defining the problem the product must solve. The second is developing a great solution to that problem. UI Tenets & Traps addresses the second part of this equation; they are about ‚Äúhow‚Äù to design a product to solve a given ‚Äúwhat‚Äù. They are about ‚Äúgetting the design right.‚Äù They are not a tool to help you determine what problem your product should solve, or to determine the vision, goals, or features for your product. Doing these things is critical, and there are great resources that can help you do them better, but UI Tenets & Traps is not one of them. To be very specific on this point, you will not find a Trap called ‚Äúmissing functionality‚Äù. This is not because missing functionality isn‚Äôt an issue. It‚Äôs because ‚Äúmissing functionality‚Äù is about ‚Äòwhat‚Äô not ‚Äòhow‚Äô.

### The 9 Tenets
- UNDERSTANDABLE : I know what I can do / I know what I‚Äôve done 
- COMFORTABLE : Interaction is physically effortless 
- RESPONSIVE : I don‚Äôt wait 
- EFFICIENT : I do less 
- ACCURATE : Information is relevant and factual 
- FORGIVING : I can undo actions 
- PROTECTIVE : My data and info are safe 
- HABITUATING : I quickly achieve mastery 
- BEAUTIFUL : It‚Äôs aesthetically pleasing 

### The 27 Traps
- Name: INVISIBLE ELEMENT
- Parent Tenet: UNDERSTANDABLE
- Definition: No label, icon, or other interface element is provided to let the user know how to achieve a goal, and the user has insufficient prior learning to overcome its absence. 
- Applies to: Hidden swipe actions, Press-and-hold actions, Hand gestures, Hidden scroll bars, Voice assistants without screens, etc. 
- Example 1: Several Tesla models included unlabeled mechanical door releases for use when power was lost to the electric door switches. The invisibility of these releases caused drivers to become trapped in their cars after accidents. In the mid-2020s, Tesla began redesigning the controls to add labels and/or integrate the electrical and mechanical controls. 
- Example 2: In 2017, the Hulu mobile app home provided no explicit indication to the user that they could scroll vertically on the home screen. As a result, some users failed to discover this capability. In a subsequent update, they added a visible ‚Äòpeak‚Äô at the bottom of the screen, eliminating the problem. 
- Example 3: In 2012 Microsoft released Windows 8. Unlike previous versions, Windows 8
removed a visible means to launch the Start Menu. The resulting user confusion led to the Start button's return in the next version of Windows.
- Example 4: In 2014 Apple announced ‚Äò3D‚Äô touch control which required users to press down hard on controls to invoke additional functionality. For example, when in the quick settings 3D touch provided the only way to turn on the personal hotspot. Because there is no visible cue for 3D touch, many users never discover this functionality. Apple announced they would phase out 3D touch in 2019. 
- Example 5: In 2008 BMW introduced a new M-car DCT gear selector. It lacked a visible "park" position. Instead, the user had to keep their foot on the brake while shutting the car down, after which the car puts itself into park. This system has since been recalled. 
- Notes: Not all things that are invisible are bad. It is usually when the only way to perform an action is invisible and there is no mechanism for teaching the user about it. For example shortcuts that have a visible analog such as Cntl+C and Cntl+V for copy and paste. Shortcuts can be invisible because they have a visual analog ‚Äìand simply provide an accelerator. 

- Name: EFFECTIVELY INVISIBLE ELEMENT 
- Parent Tenet: UNDERSTANDABLE
- Definition: A label, icon, or other interface element goes unnoticed because it is unexpected or misaligned with the user‚Äôs focus of attention. 
- Applies to: Peripherally located elements, Unlooked for elements, Unusual or non-standard elements, etc. 
- Example 1: ‚ÄúYou're on mute!‚Äù became a familiar cry among users of Zoom and other meeting apps in the early 2020s. Users frequently speak while muted because the mute indicator appears away from where people normally attend when listening to others: their faces. 
- Example 2: In a past version of the Xbox 360 interface, the global search function was placed on the controller's Y button. This was indicated in a corner of the interface but was effectively invisible to users, whose focus was on the tiles. In this example, the Y-button indicator was effectively a ‚Äògorilla‚Äô - something unexcepted by the user, while the tiles were ‚Äòbasketballs‚Äô ‚Äì the things users were focused on. A subsequent design change that placed the search function on a tile made this problem go away. 
- Example 3: In early 2019 Oculus VR set up screen had a code that needed to be put into a smartphone to activate the software. Unfortunately the code was placed in the lower right corner where users did not expect it resulting in many support calls. This issue was fixed later in 2019 by placing the code in the middle of the screen where the user is attending. 
- Example 4: At the 2017 Oscars, the Best Picture presenters were mistakenly handed the envelope for Best Actress. The card inside showed the name of the actress and the title of the movie she was in - also a Best Picture nominee. The presenters only noticed what they expected - the movie title - and announced the wrong winner. Chaos ensued. A 2018 redesign eliminated the trap.

- Name: DISTRACTION
- Parent Tenet: UNDERSTANDABLE
- Definition: Something in the interface draws the user's attention away from their current goal. 
- Applies to: Motion in the user‚Äôs periphery, Auto-playing audio or video, Pop-up notifications, Animated ads, etc. 
- Example 1: Netflix introduced updated ‚Äòautoplay‚Äô functionality to its TV app in 2024. It automatically played the currently displayed video after a brief delay. The sudden onset of loud audio and moving images distracted users trying to attend to other information on the screen.‚ÄØ 
- Example 2: The iPhone text messaging notifications can pop up over the top of the mapping application when the user is driving. This obscures the driving directions and can lead to danger. 
- Example 3: In 2019 Oculus VR introduced screen casting so that others could see what the player was doing. The system would give feedback that the game was casting via a red dot in the middle of their interface, even during immersive games. This is very distracting, spawning whole reddit threads to remove the dot. 
- Example 4: Preceding the crashes of two Boeing 737 flights in 2019, at least six simultaneous streams of visual, audible, and touch warning information competed for the pilots' attention. The system was working as designed. The distraction made it impossible for the pilots to effectively respond, resulting in the deaths of 346 people.

- Name: UNCOMPREHENDED ELEMENT 
- Parent Tenet: UNDERSTANDABLE
- Definition: A label, icon, or other interface element is noticed, but its meaning or required method of interaction is unclear. 
- Applies to: Unfamiliar icons, labels. or controls, Unfamiliar terms or language, Unfamiliar tones or sounds, Unanswerable questions, etc. 
- Example 1: In the early 2020s, Amazon changed the microphone button icon on the Fire TV remote to a branded Alexa symbol. The unfamiliar symbol confused users leading Amazon to quickly revert to the readily comprehensible microphone icon. 
- Example 2: In 2016 Waze changed their search icon from a silhouette of their logo to the very familiar and readily comprehended magnifying glass search icon. 
- Example 3: In 2019 Oculus VR users would ignore an icon that looked like the Oculus O to go "Home". This despite it being labeled "Home".
- Example 4: In 2018 Oculus Rift had a skewmorphic home space. Different options were represented in the home. For example, to edit the users avatar the users had to find a mirror to look into. This was very difficult to understand, and in later versions has been changed to labeled buttons solving the issue.
- Example 5: In 2017, the Hulu mobile app UI included many controls that were represented solely with icons. In 2019, many of these icons were replaced with less ambiguous text labels. 
- Example 6: In 2019 the iPhone nested most of the options for deleting, flagging, and moving messages under the reply function. The reply function is represented by an arrow. This in no way communicates deleting, flagging or moving messages.

- Name: INVITING DEAD END 
- Parent Tenet: UNDERSTANDABLE
- Definition: A label, icon, or other interface element is easily mistaken for the correct means of achieving a goal; it looks right, but is wrong. 
- Applies to: Similar-looking icons, Buttons that trigger ‚Äòinvalid action‚Äô messages, Labels designed to mislead users into action, etc. 
- Example 1: In 2020, Google redesigned its suite of app icons to share a four-color palette with the intention of linking the apps under one brand. Due to the resulting visual similarity, many users reported that they continually selected the wrong icons. 
- Example 2: On the original iPhone, users would get drawn into the iTunes app instead of the iPod app due to the design of the icon. Subsequent changes to the iPod (music) icon have not mitigated this problem. 
- Example 3: In 2019 Oculus VR users would get drawn into an icon that looked like a house to go "Home". Except it wouldn't, it would go do their software library and was labeled "Library".

- Name: POOR GROUPING 
- Parent Tenet: UNDERSTANDABLE
- Applies to: Unclear visual hierarchy, Poor use of white space 
Poorly organized menu structures, etc. 
- Definition: A critical relationship between two or more interface elements is unclear. 
- Example 1: In 2022, the South by Southwest (SXSW) conference registration page provided buttons for selecting conference tracks. These buttons were equally spaced between descriptions of each track, making it unclear which button matched which block of text - the one above or below? Users had to scroll to the top of the page to be sure, or else click on one of the buttons and hope for the best. 
- Example 2: In the 2000 presidential election, 4,000 people made the error of punching the second hole on the butterfly ballot in the mistaken belief that the second hole represented the second candidate, while 19,000 people punched more than one hole. This Trap changed the outcome of the election.
- Example 3: Radio buttons whose labels are closer to each other than the other radio button options make it difficult to tell which thing should be clicked. Are the radio buttons running vertically, or horizontally? 

- Name: FORCED SYNTAX 
- Parent Tenet: UNDERSTANDABLE
- Definition: A sequence of actions cannot be completed in the order or manner the user expects or prefers. 
- Applies to: Rigid starting points for tasks, ‚ÄòWake words‚Äô that must be spoken ahead of voice interactions (e.g., ‚ÄòAlexa‚Äô. ‚ÄòSiri‚Äô), etc. 
- Example 1: To create a smart home ‚Äòroutine‚Äô in the Alexa Mobile App, users were required to select the ‚Äòroutines‚Äô action and then add a smart home device to the routine. This frustrated users who expected to be able to select a device first and then create a routine for that device. In 2023, the app was updated to support both approaches. 
- Example 2: When talking to voice-driven devices like those powered by Amazon's Alexa, users must first address the device and then say their command. But this isn't always how humans formulate sentences. "Alexa, what time is it?" works, but "What time is it, Alexa?‚Äù, doesn‚Äôt. 
- Example 3: Parking garage toll machine requires users to enter their ticket first then their credit card.

- Name: MEMORY CHALLENGE 
- Parent Tenet: UNDERSTANDABLE
- Definition: The user is required to remember information that is easy to forget. 
- Applies to: Instructions that must be recalled later, Information that must be recalled when working across apps or devices, Purchase flows that do not show which saved address will be applied Passwords, etc. 
- Example 1: Before 2018, iOS users receiving two-factor authentication codes via text had to memorize and retype them into the login screen. Apple's 'security code autofill' feature now automatically detects and suggests codes for one-tap entry, eliminating the need to hold the code in memory. 
- Example 2: Efforts to make systems secure often make them impossible to use. In this example, American Express required users to remember not only the answer to their security question but also the security question itself.
- Example 3: 'Smart elevator' systems require users to select a desired floor after and then direct them to wait for a specific elevator. Some systems flash the number or letter of the elevator they need to wait for - but this information is easy to forget. 
- Example 4: Meta Ray Ban Display smartglasses are controlled by gestures via the Neural Band. There are over 9 gestures, and despite teaching users what these gestures are, they are often forgotten quickly and beyond a couple core gestures few are frequently used.

- Name: FEEDBACK FAILURE 
- Parent Tenet: UNDERSTANDABLE
- Definition: The system fails to communicate to the user the consequence of their actions, or how‚ÄØto‚ÄØresolve a failed‚ÄØaction. 
- Applies to: Lack of feedback, Ambiguous feedback, False precision in feedback, Lack of guidance for resolving errors, etc. 
- Example 1: Originally, Meta Quest VR headsets required users to manually draw safety boundaries, so hazard detection feedback depended on how accurately a user could draw. If a user mapped the space incorrectly or something entered the space (like a pet) injuries could result. Now, the system automatically determines safe zones and alerts users to obstacles and environmental changes. 
- Example 2: Reddit has a sign-up page like many websites do that asks for your email address. In the past, if you made a mistake with your email you would have to wait till you hit submit to get an error. Now Reddit tells you if you have a valid email address before you hit submit in real time. 
- Example 3: Sooner or later, everyone encounters an error. The hope is that the error will help guide the user to a solution. Feedback messages that do not do this are not helpful.

- Name: PHYSICAL CHALLENGE 
- Parent Tenet: COMFORTABLE
- Definition: Some aspect of the system causes discomfort or makes it physically difficult or impossible for the user to complete actions. 
- Applies to: Uncomfortable form factors, Hard-to-reach physical controls, Tiny touch targets, Poor control-to-display ratios, Poor text legibility, Poor speech intelligibility, Most accessibility issues, violations of Fitts law, etc. 
- Example 1: Apple introduced the Vision Pro mixed reality headset in 2023. Many users found it uncomfortable to wear for extended periods due to its size and weight. In 2025, Apple updated the headband to improve weight distribution, but reports of discomfort persisted. 
- Example 2: Human finger pads are about 12 mm across on average. Not all touch controls adhere to that norm, including the version of the iPhone lock screen music controls shown above. These were difficult for users to target and were ultimately enlarged. 
- Example 3: As mobile phone screens have gotten larger, companies have been forced to introduce 'reachability' features that bring hard-to-reach controls closer to a user's thumb. 
- Example 4: Zillow's 2017 introduction of higher-contrast pricing and address information on their mobile app led to a significant increase in customer engagement. 
- Example 5: In Windows 95 the original Start menu was in the corner but the button did not extend all the way to the edge. This meant when users threw their cursors to the top they would miss the button by one pixel. Not having the button extend all the way to the edge missed taking advantage of Fitts law and having an infinitely sized button due to the pinning action of the bounded corners of the PC interface.

- Name: ACCIDENTAL ACTIVATION
- Parent Tenet: COMFORTABLE
- Definition: It‚Äôs easy for the user to unintentionally trigger an action during normal use.
- Applies to: Unintended touchscreen presses, Unintended gesture activations, Unintended physical button presses, Overloaded button actions (e.g., double taps, press & holds), etc. 
- Example 1: As of 2025, the Wayfair shopping website features a ‚Äòmega menu‚Äô that can be unintentionally invoked when a user hovers their mouse pointer over one of several tabs at the top of the screen. Doing so causes the menu to occlude the entire screen. 
- Example 2: With gesture-based systems like Kinect, it is often difficult to determine the user's intent: Is a hand gesture a navigational swipe or an effort to scratch one's ear? This makes scrolling via hand gestures prone to accidental activations.
- Example 3: Accidental screenshot are incredibly common on the iPhone X, occurring when the volume up and standby button are pressed simultaneously. In 2018, Apple attempted to mitigate the issue by disabling screenshots when powering-up, but many other common interactions still lead to accidental activations.
- Example 4: The 2016 Chrysler Monostable Gear Shifter had many issues. One was that it was easy to move the gear accidentally into the "reverse" setting rather than "park". This has led to user injuries and class action lawsuits.
- Example 5: The Meta Quest controllers used to have their home button placed centrally where it was easy for users to accidentally hit it during vigorous play sessions. This would interrupt their gameplay with the home menu. The home buttons have now been offset from center greatly reducing the accidental activations

- Name: SLOW OR NO RESPONSE
- Parent Tenet: RESPONSIVE
- Definition: The actual or perceived time it takes the system to respond exceeds what the user wants or expects. 
- Applies to: Slow touch responses, Slow handwriting responses, Slow voice responses, Variability in response times, Extended load times, Extended startup or shutdown times, etc. 
- Example 1: As of 2025, conversational responses from voice assistants such as Amazon Alexa and Google Assistant often take more than 10 times longer than the near-instantaneous responses typical in human-to-human conversation, limiting their perceived naturalness. 
- Example 2: After pressing the button to activate the Super-Bright LED Flashlight application on an Android phone, it can take up to 5 seconds for the light to actually turn on.

- Name: CAPTIVE WAIT 
- Parent Tenet: RESPONSIVE
- Definition: The system does not allow the user to advance or back out of a process at a time of their choosing. 
- Applies to: Unskippable ads on streaming services, Unskippable cutscenes in video games, Operating system updates, etc. 
- Example 1: Video games often include unskippable video sequences (called cutscenes) that advance the story. In the acclaimed game God of War Ragnar√∂k, players cannot skip these sequences. This means watching the same cutscenes repeatedly. This is especially frustrating when the user wants to re-attempt a challenging battle that follows the cutscene. 
- Example 2: YouTube often presents users with ‚Äòpre-roll‚Äô advertisements without providing a means of advancing to the content they are actually interested in.
- Example 3: Many free to play games require the user to sit through ads in order to play them

- Name: UNNECESSARY STEP
- Parent Tenet: EFFICIENT
- Definition: The number of steps a user must take to achieve a goal is greater than it needs to be. 
- Applies to: Multi-step workflows for simple tasks, Nested architectures (in lieu of ‚Äòflat‚Äô ones), Confirmation requests (in lieu of reversible actions), etc. 
- Example 1: In 2023, as part of a broader effort to streamline how users ‚Äòlike‚Äô songs and create playlists, Spotify increased the steps to 'unlike' a song from 1 to 3. Prior to this change, users could like and unlike a song by toggling a heart button. After, to like a song, users had to tap a plus icon, which became a check mark. To unlike the same song, they had to tap the check mark, tap another check mark to unselect the song from the Liked Songs list, and then tap 'Done.' 
- Example 2: The introduction of voice control options on TV systems like Xfinity X1 and Amazon Fire TV, allow users to dramatically reduce the number of steps needed to get to known content. 
- Example 3: Removal of hamburger menus in mobile and distributing more of the choices in the first page of the interface. Spotify removed their hamburger menu and instead tested the tab bar on iOS to see how it impacted user engagement. It found that users with the tab bar ended up clicking 9% more in general and 30% more on actual menu items. The tests also revealed that reducing the number of options in the tab bar to five increased the reach of Spotify‚Äôs programmed content, the company says.
- Example 4: ‚ÄúAre you sure?‚Äù dialogs. Better handled by allowing undo. 
- Example 5: In 2019 the iPhone nested most of the options for deleting, flagging, and moving messages under the reply function --putting all of these functions that used to be available one additional step away. 
- Example 6: In HR software Workday when the user is setting up their profile they have to confirm every single field with an extra step. First name (OK, Cancel), last name (Ok, Cancel), etc
- Example 7: Amazon removed the need to go through all the steps involved with a shopping cart. They gave the user the option to purchase items with one click.

- Name: INFORMATION OVERLOAD 
- Parent Tenet: EFFICIENT
- Definition: Information presented to the user is understandable but there‚Äôs more of it than there needs to be, and the excess **measurably interferes** with efficient task completion (e.g., slows scanning, hides the next step, distracts, increases error risk). 
**Important:** ‚ÄúInformation-rich‚Äù is not automatically ‚Äúoverload.‚Äù Only flag when there is **clear, demonstrable excess** relative to the user‚Äôs goal and stage.
- Applies to: 
- Wordiness / unnecessary verbosity
- Words instead of actions --when the primary things for users to do are **perform actions** rather than get information
- Redundant instructions or repeated messages (this is also Gratuitous Redundancy Trap)
- Premature detail (edge cases/secondary info shown before the main action is complete)
- Does NOT Apply To (common false positives) Do **not** flag Information Overload when:
- The space is primarily **reference content** rather than a place to take action (knowledge base, policy pages) where depth is expected.
- The task **requires detail** (e.g., comparing plans, reviewing specs, legal/medical/financial disclosures) and the information is reasonably structured.
- The interface uses **progressive disclosure** (summary first; details behind ‚ÄúLearn more,‚Äù tabs, accordions, tooltips).
- The main issues are better flagged as other Traps. Such as **unclear wording** (either Uncomprehended Element or Inviting Dead End Traps depending on context) or **poor layout** (Poor Grouping Trap), rather than ‚Äútoo much information.‚Äù

## Decision Procedure (Gated) ‚Äî Must Follow
### Gate 0 ‚Äî State context
Before judging, make sure you have this information. If you do not ask for more detail. Explicitly identify the following things:
**Primary user goals and tasks** (what the user is trying to accomplish)
**Information Gathering or Action** Is the user collecting information or are they performing an action. 
If the goal/stage cannot be reasonably inferred, do **not** confidently flag overload. At most: ‚Äúpotential risk, needs more context.‚Äù
### Gate 1 ‚Äî Evidence of excess (must meet at least one with clear UI evidence)
Only flag if there is clear evidence such as:
- **Unnecessary detail up front:** edge cases/exceptions/advanced explanations shown before the basic action is complete.
- **Redundancy:** repeated instructions, warnings, or explanations.
- **Low signal-to-noise:** long blocks of non-actionable text (explanation/background/marketing) in a task-focused moment.
- **Obscured primary action:** the main call to action/control is buried, hard to find, or visually de-emphasized by surrounding content.
- **Competing priorities:** many equal-weight call to actions/sections with no clear hierarchy, causing decision paralysis.
- **Scan failure:** user must read paragraphs to extract a simple required instruction (e.g., ‚ÄúEnter ZIP code‚Äù not immediately scannable).
- If **yes**, proceed to Gate 2.
- If **no**, do **not** proceed to Gate 2.
### Gate 2 ‚Äî Relevance check
Ask: **Is most of the information clearly relevant to completing the user‚Äôs potential goals at this step?**
- If **yes**, do **not** flag overload.
- If **no**, proceed to Gate 3.
### Gate 3 ‚Äî Subtraction test (required)
To flag overload, the evaluator must identify:
- **What specific content can be removed, condensed, deprioritized, or moved behind progressive disclosure**
- **Why removing it would not harm task success**
If no safe reduction can be proposed, **do not** flag Information Overload.

- Name: SYSTEM AMNESIA 
- Parent Tenet: EFFICIENT
- Definition: The system fails to leverage the user‚Äôs prior work, preferences, or context. 
- Applies to: Context loss in conversational AIs, Requests for previously provided information, Recommendations to buy content you already own, etc. 
- Example 1: Patients are often asked to enter the same information into medical forms prior to every healthcare visit, even when this information has been captured in the past. 
- Example 2: Telephone customer support who ask you to provide your account info after you already provided it earlier in the call.
- Example 3: In the HR software Workday the user has to start by entering their information, like their address. However, later in the setup flow during other modules the user gets asked for their address again.

- Name: BAD PREDICTION 
- Parent Tenet: ACCURATE
- Definition: The system fails in its attempt to anticipate the user's intent, preference, or context - it guesses wrong. 
- Applies to: Irrelevant content recommendations, Poorly-timed content recommendations, Auto-correct/Auto-complete errors, etc. 
- Example 1: In 2021, Amazon‚Äôs Alexa voice assistant added unwanted 'By the way...' promotions after responses, failing to predict when users were open to these suggestions. User complaints led Amazon to reduce these prompts and allow users to turn them off altogether. 
- Example 2: Spelling autocorrection services on smartphones often make mistakes. When wrong, it is irritating, embarrassing, or insulting. 
- Example 3: Users of Netflix often binge watch episodes. There is a feature that takes the user back they left off, a great idea when it works. Unfortunately, the system often incorrectly places the user either too many episodes back or too many episodes ahead ruining things not yet revealed in the show. Then the user has to remember which episode they were actually on. 
- Example 4: "Amazon thinks my recent humidifer purchase was merely the inagural move in a newfound hoppy of humidifier collecting." This tweet from Justin Shanes highlights the negative impact bad prediction can have on user experience.

- Name: UNWANTED DISCLOSURE 
- Parent Tenet: PROTECTIVE
- Definition: The system exposes personal data or behavior in a way that is harmful, embarrassing, or unexpected. 
- Applies to: Sharing-by-default privacy settings, Audible indicators of action that cannot be silenced, Video or audio capture without consent, etc.

- Name: GRATUITOUS REDUNDANCY 
- Parent Tenet: HABITUATING
- Definition: Multiple instances of the same interface element are presented to the user at the same time. 
- Applies to: Duplicate links, Duplicate controls, Duplicate status indicators, etc. 
- Considerations: This trap can be confused with flexible syntax and invisible redundancy (which is fine). So the ability to flexibly start a multi step task from two different points is fine. This happens when there is a "noun" to "verb" or a "verb" to "noun" relationship. For example on mobile devices: sending a text message by 1) going to contacts and selecting ‚Äúsend message‚Äù or 2) going to the composer, entering a message, and selecting the recipient --this is fine. What would not be fine is two visible entrance points to the sample place (e.g. two contact app icon/visible entry points on the same screen). Likewise in Windows and Mac OS, there are many consistent invisible shortcuts for commonly used functions like Ctl + C for ‚Äúcopy‚Äù. This is fine because the shortcuts are not visible.
- Example 1: As of 2025, the homepage for the Harvard teaching hospital, Mass General, presented users with more than a dozen duplicated links, some of which appeared as many as four times. This presented users with unnecessary choices, while pushing other relevant content off screen. 
- Example 2: On Blackberry devices: two instances of a ‚ÄúNew message‚Äù notification icon were sometimes presented on the home screen at the same time.
- Example 3: On Blackberry devices: menu items can be selected directly or by going through the options menu and choosing the select option from the same view.
- Example 4: In 2014 Healthcare.gov had three links on the homepage that all went to the exact same place. They subsequently added a fourth link to the same place, which only exacerbated the issue. This duplication of choices impedes habituation.
- Example 5: As of 2020 prominent northwest medical site Swedish has massive redundancy of options which makes the site much bigger than it needs to be. For example, the "find a location" link appears 6 times on the home screen. In total, more than 30 links are duplicated. 
- Example 6: In 2024 the Washington State Department of transportation website had duplicate links for most of the options on the front page. They redesigned the site so that there was only one link to each space. This greatly reduced decision time, reduced the clutter, improved the readability at the same time.
- Example 7: Waze used to have 2+ search buttons that did the same thing on the same screen, they have now fixed this.

- Name: VARIABLE OUTCOME 
- Parent Tenet: HABITUATING
- Definition: The system responds differently and unexpectedly to the same user action at different times. 
- Applies to: Mode-dependent behavior (e.g., Caps Lock), Inconsistent support of functionality (e.g., right-click support for some instances of the same interface element, but not others), etc. 
- Example 1: The 2016 Jeep Grand Cherokee's monostable shifter always returned to a center position after every shift. The same forward or backward push could put the vehicle in Park, Drive, or another gear depending on the current state. Some drivers exited the vehicle believing they were in Park when they weren't, causing rollaway accidents and deaths. This led to a recall and redesign. 
- Example 2: The iPhone's Undo function works sometimes, and sometimes it doesn't making it difficult to habituate to.
- Example 3: The browser Back button in Twitter yields a different outcome depending on when the user clicks on it. After launching a Twitter dialog and then hitting Back, the user is taken back two steps instead of one. This lack of consistency impedes habituation. 
- Example 4: Prior to 2021 Apple introduced the touch bar to their keyboards. This was a digital strip which would show different digital buttons depending on where the user was in the interface. The issue was that globally necessary controls would move or dissappear. For example the Escape button (Esc) would not always be available even though it was always needed. As a result the 2021 MacBook finally removed the touch bar because of all the variable outcomes it caused
- Example 5: The caps lock button places the user into a mode where all letters typed are capitalized. Users often forget they are in this mode and are surprised when they still type in all caps.
- Example 6: During the development of the game Sunset Overdrive the team had a choice to make around how the controls would work via the Xbox controller. Part of the game required users to both jump to reach areas of the game and also required the user to "attach" to certain surfaces in order to do things like slide on rails to travel. There were two options: place both options on the A button or put one option on the A button and another on the X button. During user testing when both options were put on the A button users made more errors, took longer to negotiate obstacles and had less fun. The reason was that since A did both jumping and attaching sometimes the user when jump when they wished to attach to things, and sometimes users would attach when they wished to jump. The team instead placed jump on A and attach to X --which solved all the issues.
